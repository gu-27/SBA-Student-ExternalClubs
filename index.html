Student External Engagement Framework

One framework, three ways to read it. Look through each, then tell us what works best.

What this framework is for

This framework guides any student group that wants to engage with external entities (clients, partners, and the public) while using the Gonzaga and School of Business name and resources. Its goal is not to restrict but to be responsible: when a group goes external it stops being an internal club and starts representing the university, and that carries the same liabilities any consultancy carries. It aims to mitigate that risk while protecting the autonomy students value, resting on one central insight: planning an engagement is itself the risk mitigation.

=

Options A and B are identical in philosophy, faculty role, cross-cutting requirements, preparation model, and planning document. They differ in exactly one thing: how the framework is organized, and that single difference lives in Section 3 of each document.

Three ways into the same framework

Option A

Engagement Tiers

Sorts engagements by what the group is doing into three escalating tiers: exposure and public voice, then research and consulting, then binding or confidential (NDA) work. A group finds its tier, and higher tiers inherit the requirements of lower ones and add to them.

Good at An intuitive on-ramp. A group sees only the requirements that apply to it, with complexity introduced as the work escalates.
Strains Mixed engagements. Work that spans more than one type at once (say, public voice plus confidential data) has to be mapped across tiers.

Option B

One Lifecycle + Classification

Runs every engagement through the same lifecycle with the same gates, and manages risk by classifying information into four sensitivity levels and scaling oversight intensity with exposure. The process does not change; how hard the gates are pressed does.

Good at One consistent process. The same path fits every engagement and extends cleanly as a group matures and takes on higher-exposure work.
Strains Up-front load. More to absorb at the start, since even a simple engagement runs the full lifecycle and classification model.

Perspectives

Three Lenses

Reads the framework from three seats, the administrator, the faculty champion, and the student, showing what each one gains and what each one risks in the same engagement. A way to see one opportunity from every side before deciding how to organize it.

Good at Shared understanding. It surfaces what is at stake for everyone at the table, in their own terms.
Strains Not a structure. It does not organize the work; you still run engagements through Option A or Option B.
