# Student External Engagement Framework
## Option B – One Lifecycle + Information Classification

**School of Business Administration**
**Structure:** Every engagement runs through the *same* lifecycle with the same gates; risk is managed by classifying **information** into sensitivity levels and by scaling oversight intensity with exposure.

> **How to read this vs. Option A:** Options A and B share the same philosophy, the same faculty role, the same cross-cutting requirements, the same preparation model, and the same planning document. They differ in *one* thing: how the framework is organized. Option A sorts engagements by **what the group is doing** (exposure, consulting, binding/confidential work). Option B runs a single lifecycle and sorts **information** by sensitivity. Read both; the difference is structural, not cosmetic.

---

## 1. Purpose & Philosophy

This framework guides any student group that engages, or wants to engage, with **external entities** using the university's or School of Business's name and resources.

The goal is not to restrict. It is to be responsible. When a group goes external, it stops being an internal club and starts representing Gonzaga to the outside world. That is a real opportunity for student learning, for the community, and for the university's reputation, and it carries the same liabilities any consultancy carries.

Two principles hold the whole thing together:

1. **Mitigate risk while enabling innovation.** Too little structure breeds impulsive errors; too much kills the autonomy and creativity students value. This framework aims for the middle: clear guidelines, not bureaucracy.
2. **There needs to be an adult in the room.** A faculty champion is what makes an engagement sustainable and accountable. Faculty involvement is self-selecting (nobody is forced to advise), but if a group does external work, this framework defines what that partnership requires.

**Dual mandate.** Student development and external-partner impact are co-equal. Neither may be optimized into the ground, and "I am here to learn" never lowers the professional bar.

**The reframe:** treated like a real consultancy, with liability understood and risk deliberately mitigated, this discipline *becomes* the strength and the differentiator. "We trust you to do this, and we are going to help you do it well."

**The central insight: planning is the mitigation.** The most common failure mode is impulsivity. Groups wing it, skip the planning, and that is what breeds most errors. So this framework is a thinking tool as much as a rulebook. Requiring a group to plan an engagement *is* the risk mitigation. Faculty's job is largely to review that thinking and say "you are ready" or "you are missing something."

## 2. The Faculty Role (specified first, because everything depends on it)

The adult role is the load-bearing element, so it is defined before anything else. It is a small set of **non-delegable gates** plus recorded judgment, designed so students run day-to-day operations while faculty holds the boundary.

**The four hard gates.** Four categories always return to the faculty champion, at any point in the lifecycle:

1. Anything external to the university.
2. Anything binding or strategic.
3. Anything financial (all expenses and engagement terms).
4. The group's outbound public voice (down to a single marketing post).

Rationale: students have essentially zero experience in these four areas, so the line must be crisp.

**Authority is scope-gated, never positional.** No student, including the most senior officer, may act externally on title alone. A written, approved scope carries the authority to act within it. Absent a scope, the same action has no standing and routes upward.

**Students draft; the institution sends.** Students prepare outreach, agreements, and materials; a designated leader or the institution issues them. Students do not commit the group to anything.

**Escalation fails safe.** Every role has a written escalation path terminating at the faculty champion. When a student leadership seat is vacant, its oversight collapses **upward**, never sideways and never into nothing.

**Faculty is not a bottleneck.** These gates only stay sustainable on top of a real student-supervision layer. If the student supervision is thin, the adult absorbs the gap and becomes the bottleneck. Building the student supervision layer is part of standing up any group.

## 3. The Operating Model (Option B's spine)

Rather than sorting engagements into different playbooks, Option B runs every engagement through **one lifecycle**, manages risk by **classifying information**, and scales **oversight intensity** with exposure.

### 3A. The single engagement lifecycle

Every external engagement, whatever its subject, moves through the same stages, each with a defined gate:

| Stage | Who owns it | Gate |
|---|---|---|
| Prospect & qualify | A named student role drafts; a leader sends | Faculty cc'd on all outreach |
| Scope | Assigned leader, with the partner | Scope approved by senior student leader **and** faculty before it is confirmed externally; written and specific enough to say "no" with |
| Handoff | Scoping owner to delivery lead | Written brief: partner context, expectations, scope guardrails, cadence, top two risks |
| Execution | Delivery lead, with day-to-day autonomy | A supervisor attends regular meetings and is cc'd on partner comms; two syncs at phase boundaries |
| Interim / midpoint | Delivery lead | Held to a written standard *distinct* from the final (direction-setting, not a rough final) |
| Final | Delivery lead | Internal practice + faculty practice before the partner sees it |
| Pre-release | Faculty or senior leader | Short fixed checklist (accuracy, partner-readiness, branding, links; ~30 min) before anything reaches the partner |
| Closeout | Delivery lead | Deliver, thank, survey the partner (satisfaction only), capture reflections, transfer the relationship to the institution |

Oversight is continuous by presence and cc, not triggered after the fact.

### 3B. Information classification (the risk model)

Information is sorted into four levels *before it is written down or stored*, and each level has a fixed home:

| Level | Content | Where it may live |
|---|---|---|
| 1 – Public | Approved marketing, consented testimonials | Public surfaces, curated |
| 2 – Internal | Roles, processes, factual profiles, non-confidential partner records | Shared internal systems |
| 3 – Restricted | Application scores, evaluations, incident records | University systems only; a pointer, never the content, in shared systems |
| 4 – Confidential / NDA | Partner data under NDA | Restricted institutional storage; a stub and pointer only |

Three rules make this hold:
- **Refuse even if offered.** Never write Level 3 or 4 content into a shared system, even if someone pastes it in. Refuse, name the level, and offer to store a Level-2 pointer instead. This is the backstop for human error.
- **Quarantine confidential work.** NDA-covered material cannot live in shared tools, cannot be read by systems serving other engagements, and any rollup must strip partner-identifying detail.
- **Public disclosure is a cleared gate.** A partner may be named publicly only when explicitly cleared and with no confidential detail.

### 3C. Intensity scaling

The lifecycle is the same for a low-exposure engagement and a high-exposure one; what changes is *how hard the gates are pressed*. Exposure rises with binding commitments, confidential information (Level 3 or 4), financial terms, and public visibility. Higher exposure means faculty risk assessment before scope, counsel review of any binding language with the institution as signatory, required conduct training with documented completion, and enrollment in a course structure to limit liability.

**Honest caveat:** the top of the classification model (Level 4 / NDA) is designed, not proven. Stand up and rehearse it (stub records, restricted storage, refusal drill) *before* the first confidential engagement, not when it arrives.

## 4. Cross-Cutting Requirements (apply across the whole lifecycle)

**Communication and touchpoint plan.** Identify every point of external contact (outreach, scope agreement, status updates, deliverable handoff, closeout) and provide a template or standard for each, so tone and quality stay consistent and nobody oversells what the group can do. Faculty reviews the touchpoints.

**Records are the accountability system, and a closeout gate.** Keep engagement records (who, what scope, what outcome, links to deliverables) in a version-controlled or otherwise attributed system where every change has an author and a date. Critically: make the scope / outcomes / lessons record a *condition of closing* an engagement. Optional documentation stays empty. Audit the gap between what the framework requires and what actually got filled once per term.

**Student-privacy mirror.** The inward-facing twin of partner confidentiality, and it is Levels 3 and 4 of the classification model pointed at your own students. Application scores, evaluations, and rankings live only in university systems; shared records hold facts, not judgments; no cross-term performance dossiers. External parties never rate individual students. Confirm FERPA posture with Gonzaga IT and general counsel before any student-data design ships.

**Custody and continuity resolved up front.** Institutional (not personal) account ownership, named storage homes for each level, and credentials stored by reference only. Partner relationships transfer from the individual student to the institution at closeout, so continuity and liability do not walk out with a graduate.

## 5. Preparation & Competency

- **Vet with adult decision authority.** Students may administer recruiting; selection decisions sit with the leadership plus faculty layer.
- **Progressive exposure.** Internal or low-stakes leadership (for example an internal competition) before external-facing leadership. First-time leads get a named support mechanism, because the first-time-lead gap is structural, not an anomaly.
- **Cadenced, not front-loaded, training.** Deliver training just in time against lifecycle milestones rather than in one overwhelming block. Cap mandatory training time: over-programming is itself a failure mode for students carrying a full course load.
- **Protect the learning.** If AI assistance is available, phase-gate it so students build core skills unassisted first, and enforce the gate in the system rather than by willpower.
- **Teach the failure modes honestly.** Document how first-timers in each role actually struggle and the counter-moves, framed as growth edges, not verdicts.

---

## 6. The Master Planning Document

Before going external, a group completes one coherent planning document that walks it through everything above. It is simultaneously the group's plan, the faculty review artifact, and the university's due-diligence record. It forces the group to articulate:

1. Scope (what exactly are we doing and delivering), specific enough to say "no" with.
2. The highest information level the engagement will touch (1 through 4), and where each kind of information will live.
3. Exposure assessment (binding, financial, confidential, or public elements) and the resulting oversight intensity.
4. Counterparty profile.
5. Student capability check (faculty-validated).
6. Partner expectation-setting plan.
7. Communication and touchpoint plan with templates.
8. Public / social strategy, if applicable.
9. Lifecycle checkpoints and faculty sign-off.

---

## 7. Sustaining It

- **Expect the ambition cycle.** New groups over-commit early and correct later. Build the correction into the calendar rather than rediscovering it.
- **A skipped review is zero reviews.** Any gate that was interrupted or not actually run is a HOLD, not a pass. Never let an empty result read as a clean bill.
- **Audit designed vs. lived once a term.** Check which gates actually ran, which records actually filled, and which documents drifted from reality. Record reality rather than silently reconciling.
