# Student External Engagement Framework
## Perspectives View: Three Lenses on the Same Opportunity

**School of Business Administration**

This is not a fourth version of the framework and it does not compete with Option A or Option B. It is a **lens layer** that works with either. The same opportunity and the same risks look different depending on where you sit. Before deciding *how* the framework is organized, it helps to see *what is at stake for each person reading it*.

Read the lens that fits your seat, or read all three to understand what everyone else is weighing.

---

## The Administrator's Lens
*(dean, associate dean, associate provost, general counsel, the institution)*

**The opportunity through this lens.** Well-run student external engagement is a differentiator. It is experiential learning at scale, a steady source of industry and community goodwill, a recruiting and alumni story, and a clean fit with the school's mission of forming students through service. A pipeline of students doing real work for real organizations is an institutional asset.

**The exposure through this lens.** A student group is not seen as a club by the outside world; it is seen as Gonzaga. That means the institution carries the reputational blast radius of every engagement, the contractual and financial liability of any binding commitment, the compliance weight of student data (FERPA), and the precedent problem: a rule written for one club tends to apply to all of them. The quietest risk is a single point of failure, where the whole enterprise rests on one faculty member's personal effort.

**What an administrator should consider.** Is there institutional liability coverage or indemnification for student external work, and who is authorized to sign anything binding? Does this scale across many groups without becoming unmanageable? Is there a consequence structure, and who owns enforcement? What is our FERPA and counsel posture before any student-data design ships? And what precedent are we setting for every future group?

**The reframe.** The rigor in this framework is not friction. It is what makes student engagement defensible, insurable, and repeatable. Ungoverned engagement is a latent liability; governed engagement is a durable asset.

**The one question to keep asking:** *If this goes wrong at the worst possible client, are we protected, and can we show we did our due diligence?*

## The Faculty Lens
*(the champion or adviser)*

**The opportunity through this lens.** This is high-impact teaching. You watch students grow faster here than anywhere else, you build real relationships with industry, and you create something that outlasts any single course. For many faculty it is the most rewarding work they do.

**The exposure through this lens.** You are the named boundary-owner. When the student supervision layer is thin, you absorb the gap, and the role quietly slides from strategic oversight into doing the work yourself. That is the bottleneck failure mode, and it is the most common way this kind of program burns out its adult. There is also personal reputational exposure: if your group embarrasses the school, it lands partly on you, which creates a constant pull between honoring student autonomy and protecting quality.

**What faculty should consider.** What exactly am I signing up for, in terms of the gates I own versus what students run day to day? Is there a real student-supervision structure beneath me, or will everything route to me? How do I set expectations so I am reviewing student thinking rather than producing the deliverable? And what is my succession plan if I step back, so the program does not depend on me alone?

**The reframe.** Your job is the gates and the judgment, not the labor. The framework exists so you can say yes to advising without it consuming you. Faculty belongs at the strategic center, not the operational center.

**The one question to keep asking:** *Can I hold the boundary without becoming the bottleneck?*

## The Student Lens
*(group members and student leaders)*

**The opportunity through this lens.** This is the real thing: actual client work, a portfolio, a network, leadership reps, and the kind of experience that gets you hired. You get to do something that genuinely matters for a real organization, and you get to represent Gonzaga while doing it.

**The exposure through this lens.** The moment you go external, you stop being a club and you *are* Gonzaga to that client. A mistake can cost a real business real money or embarrass the university, and neither is easy to undo. This is also why you cannot commit the group or speak on the strength of a title alone. The guardrails are not a sign that anyone distrusts you; they are the thing that lets you take on bigger, riskier, more impressive work without it blowing up.

**What students should consider.** Why does each gate exist, and how does it actually enable ambition rather than restrict it? What can I decide on my own versus what needs sign-off? Am I inside my scope? Am I in over my head, and is it safe to say so early? Most of all: am I planning this, or am I about to wing it? Impulsivity, not lack of talent, is what causes most of the failures here.

**The reframe.** Autonomy is earned through planning and demonstrated competence. The discipline is what makes you look like professionals instead of unprepared students. Thinking it through *is* the work.

**The one question to keep asking:** *Have I actually thought this through, or am I about to wing it?*
