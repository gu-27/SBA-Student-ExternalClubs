# Student External Engagement Framework
## Option A – Organized by Engagement Type

**School of Business Administration**
**Structure:** Groups self-identify the *type* of engagement they are doing; each type carries its own escalating set of requirements.

> **How to read this vs. Option B:** Options A and B share the same philosophy, the same faculty role, the same cross-cutting requirements, the same preparation model, and the same planning document. They differ in *one* thing: how the framework is organized. Option A sorts engagements by **what the group is doing** (exposure, consulting, binding/confidential work). Option B runs a single lifecycle and sorts **information** by sensitivity. Read both; the difference is structural, not cosmetic.

---

## 1. Purpose & Philosophy

This framework guides any student group that engages, or wants to engage, with **external entities** using the university's or School of Business's name and resources.

The goal is not to restrict. It is to be responsible. When a group goes external, it stops being an internal club and starts representing Gonzaga to the outside world. That is a real opportunity for student learning, for the community, and for the university's reputation, and it carries the same liabilities any consultancy carries.

Two principles hold the whole thing together:

1. **Mitigate risk while enabling innovation.** Too little structure breeds impulsive errors; too much kills the autonomy and creativity students value. This framework aims for the middle: clear guidelines, not bureaucracy.
2. **There needs to be an adult in the room.** A faculty champion is what makes an engagement sustainable and accountable. Faculty involvement is self-selecting (nobody is forced to advise), but if a group does external work, this framework defines what that partnership requires.

**Dual mandate.** Student development and external-partner impact are co-equal. Neither may be optimized into the ground, and "I am here to learn" never lowers the professional bar.

**The reframe:** treated like a real consultancy, with liability understood and risk deliberately mitigated, this discipline *becomes* the strength and the differentiator. "We trust you to do this, and we are going to help you do it well."

**The central insight: planning is the mitigation.** The most common failure mode is impulsivity. Groups wing it, skip the planning, and that is what breeds most errors. So this framework is a thinking tool as much as a rulebook. Requiring a group to plan an engagement *is* the risk mitigation. Faculty's job is largely to review that thinking and say "you are ready" or "you are missing something."

## 2. The Faculty Role (specified first, because everything depends on it)

The adult role is the load-bearing element, so it is defined before anything else. It is a small set of **non-delegable gates** plus recorded judgment, designed so students run day-to-day operations while faculty holds the boundary.

**The four hard gates.** Four categories always return to the faculty champion, regardless of tier:

1. Anything external to the university.
2. Anything binding or strategic.
3. Anything financial (all expenses and engagement terms).
4. The group's outbound public voice (down to a single marketing post).

Rationale: students have essentially zero experience in these four areas, so the line must be crisp.

**Authority is scope-gated, never positional.** No student, including the most senior officer, may act externally on title alone. A written, approved scope carries the authority to act within it. Absent a scope, the same action has no standing and routes upward.

**Students draft; the institution sends.** Students prepare outreach, agreements, and materials; a designated leader or the institution issues them. Students do not commit the group to anything.

**Escalation fails safe.** Every role has a written escalation path terminating at the faculty champion. When a student leadership seat is vacant, its oversight collapses **upward**, never sideways and never into nothing.

**Faculty is not a bottleneck.** These gates only stay sustainable on top of a real student-supervision layer. If the student supervision is thin, the adult absorbs the gap and becomes the bottleneck. Building the student supervision layer is part of standing up any group.

## 3. The Engagement Tiers (Option A's spine)

A group identifies which tier its engagement falls into. Higher tiers inherit the requirements of lower tiers and add to them.

### Tier 1 – Exposure & Public Voice
*Guest speakers, industry panels, "career glimpse" events, and any public-facing use of the Gonzaga / SBA name (social posts, press). No client work, no contracts.*

Primary risk: reputational alignment. Is this speaker, topic, or post aligned with the university's values, and could external parties object?

Requirements:
- Faculty champion vets the speaker, topic, and person before invitation; routes to associate dean or dean approval.
- For public voice: the group develops a lightweight communication strategy first (goals, audience, voice, what could go wrong). Faculty signs off on the strategy. After sign-off, the group has freedom to operate within it, with faculty monitoring that posts stay on point.

### Tier 2 – Research & Consulting
*Data gathering, analysis, reports, problem-solving, or implementation for an external client. No binding contract or confidential-information obligation.*

Primary risks: reputation (quality and professionalism) and harm to the client (bad or misused work).

Requirements (inherit Tier 1, add):
- **Defined scope** document so everyone knows what is being delivered, specific enough to say "no" with, and restated at every meeting.
- **Capability check** performed by faculty, not by the students themselves. Students cannot be the judge of their own competency.
- **Client expectation-setting**: an explicit conversation that these are students and outcomes may be rough. Temper expectations up front.
- **Ongoing monitoring**: a supervisor attends the group's regular meetings and is cc'd on client communications. Not surveillance; presence.
- **Rehearsal before exposure**: at least one internal practice and one faculty/adviser practice before any client-facing presentation. Interim and final deliverables are held to *different, written* standards (a midpoint is a direction-setting conversation, not a rough final).
- **Pre-release quality gate**: a short fixed checklist (accuracy against the analysis, client-readiness, branding, working links; roughly 30 minutes) run before any deliverable reaches the client.

Intensity scales with complexity and counterparty. Deeper or operational work raises the bar on each requirement, but does not invent new ones.

### Tier 3 – Binding or Confidential (NDA)
*Any engagement with an NDA or other binding agreement, or handling of client-confidential information.*

Primary risk: contractual and legal liability, not just reputation.

Requirements (inherit Tier 2, add):
- **Faculty and dean risk assessment** first: why is there an NDA, what is the benefit, what is at stake, what happens if it is breached.
- **Counsel review** of the agreement language, with the institution (not a student) as signatory.
- **Students sign an acknowledgment** and are placed in a course structure to limit liability.
- **Required conduct training** on how to hold information and how not to breach the agreement, with **documented completion** as the due-diligence record.
- **Confidential information never enters shared systems.** Store a pointer to where it lives (restricted institutional storage), never the content itself, and refuse to record it even if someone offers it.

**Honest caveat:** this pathway is designed, not proven. It should be rehearsed (stub records, restricted storage, refusal drill) *before* the first confidential engagement arrives, not improvised when it does.

## 4. Cross-Cutting Requirements (apply at every tier)

**Communication and touchpoint plan.** Identify every point of external contact (outreach, scope agreement, status updates, deliverable handoff, closeout) and provide a template or standard for each, so tone and quality stay consistent and nobody oversells what the group can do. Faculty reviews the touchpoints.

**Records are the accountability system, and a closeout gate.** Keep engagement records (who, what scope, what outcome, links to deliverables) in a version-controlled or otherwise attributed system where every change has an author and a date. Critically: make the scope / outcomes / lessons record a *condition of closing* an engagement. Optional documentation stays empty. Audit the gap between what the framework requires and what actually got filled once per term.

**Student-privacy mirror.** The inward-facing twin of client confidentiality. Application scores, evaluations, and rankings live only in university systems; shared records hold facts, not judgments; no cross-term performance dossiers. External parties never rate individual students. Confirm FERPA posture with Gonzaga IT and general counsel before any student-data design ships.

**Custody and continuity resolved up front.** Institutional (not personal) account ownership, named storage homes for each kind of record, and credentials stored by reference only. Client relationships transfer from the individual student to the institution at closeout, so continuity and liability do not walk out with a graduate.

## 5. Preparation & Competency

- **Vet with adult decision authority.** Students may administer recruiting; selection decisions sit with the leadership plus faculty layer.
- **Progressive exposure.** Internal or low-stakes leadership (for example an internal competition) before external-facing leadership. First-time leads get a named support mechanism, because the first-time-lead gap is structural, not an anomaly.
- **Cadenced, not front-loaded, training.** Deliver training just in time against engagement milestones rather than in one overwhelming block. Cap mandatory training time: over-programming is itself a failure mode for students carrying a full course load.
- **Protect the learning.** If AI assistance is available, phase-gate it so students build core skills unassisted first, and enforce the gate in the system rather than by willpower.
- **Teach the failure modes honestly.** Document how first-timers in each role actually struggle and the counter-moves, framed as growth edges, not verdicts.

---

## 6. The Master Planning Document

Before going external, a group completes one coherent planning document that walks it through everything above. It is simultaneously the group's plan, the faculty review artifact, and the university's due-diligence record. It forces the group to articulate:

1. Engagement type and tier, and scope (what exactly are we doing and delivering).
2. Risk assessment for that tier (any binding or confidential element).
3. Counterparty profile.
4. Student capability check (faculty-validated).
5. Client expectation-setting plan.
6. Communication and touchpoint plan with templates.
7. Public / social strategy, if applicable.
8. Monitoring checkpoints.
9. Faculty champion sign-off.

---

## 7. Sustaining It

- **Expect the ambition cycle.** New groups over-commit early and correct later. Build the correction into the calendar rather than rediscovering it.
- **A skipped review is zero reviews.** Any gate that was interrupted or not actually run is a HOLD, not a pass. Never let an empty result read as a clean bill.
- **Audit designed vs. lived once a term.** Check which gates actually ran, which records actually filled, and which documents drifted from reality. Record reality rather than silently reconciling.
